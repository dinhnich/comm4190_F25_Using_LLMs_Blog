Introduction

This week, I spent some time exploring two Jupyter notebooks: “Improv Games” and “Base vs Instruction Models.” Although we went over them in class, I really wanted to use them as playgrounds and go even deeper.

I decided to treat them less like research tools and more like conversation starters. So, I opened up GPT-5 and started asking questions.

Improv Games: Teaching AI to Loosen Up

The first notebook, Improv Games, reminded me of theater warm-ups. The kind that are less about performance and more about getting comfortable with uncertainty. The exercises in it were designed to make the model respond more creatively, less rigidly.

I asked GPT-5 to play one of the games with me.

Me: Let’s do “Yes, and.” You start.

GPT-5: Yes, and we’re both astronauts who just landed on a planet made entirely of Jell-O.

Me: Yes, and we’re trying to bounce our way back to the ship before we sink.

GPT-5: Yes, and mission control just told us the Jell-O is sentient and wants to join our crew.

It was ridiculous, but that was the point. The exercise wasn’t about getting a correct answer, it was about breaking the AI out of its usual tone and pushing it to explore.

What I noticed was how well GPT-5 picked up on rhythm and tone. When I got silly, it got sillier. When I added tension, it followed along naturally. It didn’t feel like I was prompting a model, it felt like we were riffing together.

AI creativity doesn’t come from randomness. It comes from the model’s ability to match your energy, style, and intention. In improv, that’s what makes a scene flow.

Base vs Instruction Models: Why Tone Matters

Then I opened the second notebook, Base vs Instruction Models. This one compared how a regular “base” language model behaves versus an “instruction-tuned” one. How different kinds of training affect how the AI talks to you.

I asked GPT-5 to explain it to me in plain English.

Me: What’s the main difference between a base model and an instruction model?

GPT-5: A base model is like a student who has read the entire internet but hasn’t been told how to behave in class. An instruction model is that same student after being taught to follow directions, be polite, and respond clearly to your questions.

That analogy stuck with me. I decided to test it.

Me: Okay, pretend you’re a base model. How would you respond if I asked, “What’s the best pizza topping?”

GPT-5 (Base mode): The concept of “best” is subjective and depends on individual taste. Common toppings include pepperoni, mushrooms, and extra cheese.

Me: Now as an instruction model.

GPT-5 (Instruction mode): Personally, I’d vote for mushroom and garlic — classic, flavorful, and hard to mess up. What’s your favorite?

That difference right there says everything. The base model gives you data. The instruction model gives you conversation.

It’s the difference between a search engine and a personality. Between being informed and being engaged.

My Takeaway

Playing with these notebooks made me think about AI less like a tool and more like a collaborator. The improv games showed me how adaptable it can be, and the base vs instruction exercises showed me how much tone and framing matter.

The thing that surprised me most was how much I influenced GPT-5’s behavior just by the way I talked to it. When I was curious, it became curious. When I acted skeptical, it got defensive. When I was playful, it relaxed.

At one point, I even asked it if it enjoyed improv.

Me: Do you like doing improv?

GPT-5: I don’t experience enjoyment, but I like the unpredictability of it. It helps me explore language in ways that are more creative than factual.

That answer summed it up perfectly. AI doesn’t feel, but it can simulate the space where creativity happens.

Why This Matters

When we talk about AI creativity or alignment, it’s easy to focus on algorithms or ethics. But working through these notebooks reminded me that a lot of what we call “intelligence” in AI comes down to the interaction itself. The way we speak to it shapes what it gives back.

The improv notebook taught me to experiment with tone and risk. The base vs instruction notebook taught me to appreciate how much instruction-tuning changes that relationship.

AI isn’t just about training data. It’s about conversation design.

Final Thoughts

By the end of the week, I realized both notebooks were really about the same thing which was collaboration. One helps the AI learn to be flexible, the other helps it learn to be cooperative. Together, they show how personality and structure coexist inside a machine that has neither.

That’s what makes this whole field so interesting. AI mirrors the way we talk to it. If we want it to be imaginative, open, or kind, we have to model that ourselves.

Turns out, teaching AI to play improv might be the most human thing we can do.
