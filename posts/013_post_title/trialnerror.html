Trial and Error

One of the most fundamental learning mechanisms explored in A Brief History of Intelligence is trial and error, a process that predates even the earliest complex nervous systems. Long before brains could form detailed representations of the world, organisms learned simply by acting, failing, and adjusting. This primitive yet powerful process set the stage for the evolution of more advanced intelligence.

Trial and error learning, sometimes called operant conditioning, is the idea that behaviors are shaped by their consequences. Actions that lead to rewards are repeated, while those that lead to punishment or failure are avoided. Bennett notes that this process emerged well before humans or even vertebrates. Early organisms developed feedback loops where successful actions increased survival odds, gradually building the scaffolding for what we now recognize as adaptive behavior.

In essence, trial and error was the first algorithm of intelligence, a biological way of searching through possible actions to find effective ones.

The Challenge of Credit and Time

However, trial and error comes with its own problems. How does an organism or an AI system determine which action led to success or failure, especially when there is a delay between cause and effect? A foraging animal might eat a new type of fruit and only hours later feel sick. How does it learn to connect that distant outcome with the earlier action?

Bennett explains that this challenge mirrors what AI researchers call the temporal credit assignment problem. When a reinforcement signal, such as a reward, arrives, the system must decide which previous actions or states deserve credit or blame. Our ancestors solved this gradually through neural mechanisms that maintained short-term memory traces of actions, allowing delayed rewards to strengthen the right synaptic connections.

This connection between biological learning and artificial intelligence shows how deeply trial and error is woven into the fabric of cognition. Evolution itself is a form of trial and error, where genetic variations tested over generations yield adaptive traits. Intelligence, in this sense, is evolution accelerated inside the brain.

From Biological Learning to Reinforcement Learning

In modern AI, reinforcement learning (RL) operates on the same principle. An agent explores an environment, performs actions, and receives feedback in the form of rewards or penalties. Over time, it learns which strategies maximize long-term reward. But just as in biological systems, this process is often slow, data-hungry, and inefficient.

Humans, on the other hand, rarely need thousands of trials to learn not to touch a hot stove. We can generalize lessons and infer cause and effect from limited experience. Bennett suggests that this leap, from brute-force trial and error to mental simulation and abstract reasoning, marks a key evolutionary breakthrough. Our brains began to simulate possible outcomes internally, allowing us to test scenarios in thought rather than in the physical world.

The Implications for AI

Trial and error remains at the heart of how machines learn. Yet, as Dennett’s “frame problem” highlights, intelligence is not just about trying things. It is about knowing which things are worth trying. AI systems still struggle to determine what information matters, which actions are relevant, and how to balance exploration with exploitation.

Humans manage this effortlessly by integrating emotion, context, and prior experience. When we hesitate before repeating a mistake, we are invoking not just memory but a deep evaluative sense that machines still lack.

Bennett’s account reminds us that trial and error was never just a primitive process. It was the seed of foresight. The leap from reacting to anticipating, from external feedback to internal modeling, is what truly distinguishes higher intelligence. Until AI systems can perform that same leap, learning not just from outcomes but from imagined possibilities, they will remain bound by the same slow, mechanical loops that governed our earliest ancestors.

Personal Reflection

Reading Bennett’s discussion of trial and error made me think about how much of my own learning still depends on this process. When I work on school projects, learn a new skill, or try to understand something complicated, I often go through a series of mistakes before I get it right. It is interesting to realize that this same pattern connects me to the earliest forms of life that also learned by testing actions and seeing what worked.

What stood out to me is that trial and error is both effective and limited. It helps us learn through experience, but it can be slow and inefficient. Humans have developed the ability to think ahead and imagine different outcomes before acting, which makes our learning faster and more flexible. AI systems, as Bennett explains, still rely heavily on trial and error. They can process more data than we can, but they often lack the insight that allows people to generalize and learn from fewer examples.

This comparison helped me understand how far human intelligence has come and how much of it is still grounded in basic feedback and repetition. It also reminded me that making mistakes is a natural and necessary part of learning, not just for early organisms or machines, but for all of us.
